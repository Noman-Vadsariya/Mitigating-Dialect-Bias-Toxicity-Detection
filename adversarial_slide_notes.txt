SLIDE 12 — Performance of Adversarial De-biasing (sweep over lambda)
================================================================
Suggested title: "Adversarial De-biasing — Sweep over lambda"

Idea:
  Train the classifier alongside an adversary that tries to predict
  dialect from the model's outputs. A gradient-reversal term
  (weight lambda) pushes the classifier to produce dialect-invariant
  predictions, enforcing Equalized Odds (equal FPR AND FNR across
  AAE / SAE) [Zhang et al., 2018].

Key Takeaways:
  - Low lambda (approx 0): model behaves like a vanilla baseline —
    large FPR gap (~0.10) and FNR gap (~0.09), high FNR on AAE (~0.31).
  - Moderate lambda (0.15 – 0.25): sweet spot. Both gaps collapse
    while F1 holds at ~0.82.
  - High lambda (0.6): training becomes unstable — FPR spikes to
    0.63 on AAE, F1 dips. Classic symptom of the adversarial
    minimax losing equilibrium.
  - Very high lambda (1.0 – 1.5): gradient reversal dominates;
    classifier collapses toward a degenerate near-uniform solution —
    gaps go to ~0 but FNR rises.
  - Fairness does NOT improve monotonically with lambda; we must
    find the stable regime.

Best Performance at lambda = 0.25 (adv_c = 3.0, thresh-tuned):
  FPR (AAE): 0.185     FPR gap: 0.033
  FNR (AAE): 0.161     FNR gap: 0.046
  F1: 82.3             Accuracy: 82.6

SLIDE 13 — Performance of Adversarial De-biasing (sweep over adv_c)
================================================================
Suggested title: "Adversarial De-biasing — Sweep over adv_c (adversary capacity)"

Idea:
  With lambda fixed at 0.25, we sweep the adversary's capacity
  (adv_c = inverse-L2 regularization of the logistic-regression
  adversary). Larger adv_c = stronger, more flexible adversary;
  smaller adv_c = weaker adversary that is easier to "fool."

Key Takeaways:
  - Weak adversary (adv_c <= 0.5): adversary can't pressure the
    classifier — F1 drops (0.77 at c=0.5) and training is noisy
    because the gradient reversal points in a low-information
    direction.
  - Mid-capacity (adv_c = 1.0): worst cell — adversary is just
    strong enough to destabilize training but not strong enough
    to converge; AAE FPR explodes to 0.52.
  - Strong adversary (adv_c = 3.0): equilibrium regime. Balanced
    FPR (0.185 vs 0.152), F1 = 0.823, both gaps small.
  - Very strong (adv_c = 10): capacity saturates; no further
    improvement, curve plateaus.
  - Unlike lambda, adv_c shows a non-monotonic / bathtub response —
    a reminder that adversarial training is about finding
    EQUILIBRIUM, not turning a knob higher.
  - adv_c has a much SMALLER direct impact on overall score than
    lambda: across our grid, adv_c alone explains only ~1% of the
    variance in F1, vs ~54% for lambda. adv_c's role is less about
    where we end up on the accuracy-fairness curve, and more about
    whether a given lambda setting converges cleanly or diverges.

Takeaway (replaces repeated "Best Performance" box from slide 12):
  adv_c = 3.0 is the smallest capacity at which adversarial training
  converges cleanly. Cells at c = 0.5 and c = 1.0 diverge (F1 < 0.81
  or FPR spike) — the right adversary capacity is as important as
  lambda. We use adv_c = 3.0 in all subsequent results.

SLIDE 14 — Impact of Threshold Tuning
================================================================

Idea:
  After adversarial training, pick a different decision threshold
  per group (t_AAE, t_SAE) on the validation set to minimize the
  FPR gap under an F1 floor. This is a post-hoc, training-free
  step equivalent to a per-group logit shift at inference — a
  cheap equalized-odds post-processing layer on top of the
  adversarial model [Hardt et al., 2016].

Key Takeaways:
  - FPR: huge win. AAE FPR halves (0.370 -> 0.185) and the FPR
    gap collapses by 0.182 (0.215 -> 0.033). SAE is essentially
    unchanged.
  - FNR: a controlled trade-off. AAE FNR rises by 0.065 —
    expected, because raising t_AAE converts some former false
    positives into new false negatives. The FNR gap shrinks
    (0.109 -> 0.046), so errors are now BALANCED across groups.
  - F1 / Accuracy: virtually free. F1 drops only 0.008 and
    accuracy 0.005.
  - Adversarial training alone barely moves the per-group rates;
    it reshapes the MARGIN DISTRIBUTION so the tuner has usable
    slack. Together they are much stronger than either in
    isolation.

One-liner takeaway (optional):
  Adversarial training aligns the score distributions; threshold
  tuning translates that alignment into fair decisions — at ~1 pt
  F1 cost.
