SLIDE 12 — Sweep over lambda
================================================================

[OPEN — frame the method, ~15 sec]
"So far you've seen the baseline XGBoost and the VS-Loss-based
debiasing results. My contribution is the adversarial branch. The
idea goes back to Zhang et al. 2018: while the main classifier is
learning toxicity, a second model — the adversary — is trying to
predict DIALECT from the classifier's outputs. A gradient-reversal
term, weighted by lambda, pushes the classifier to produce
representations the adversary CAN'T distinguish by dialect, which
is exactly the definition of Equalized Odds."

[IMPORTANT CLARIFICATION — integrated from anticipated Q&A, ~15 sec]
"One design choice I want to highlight: the adversary doesn't just
see the prediction — it sees (margin, y, margin * y). That's what
makes this EQUALIZED ODDS specifically, rather than demographic
parity. By letting the adversary condition on the true label, we
only penalize dialect information that is NOT already explained by
toxicity. This avoids the common failure mode where the model is
forced to ignore genuinely toxic content just because it correlates
with dialect."

[READ THE PLOT — ~25 sec]
"On the plot you can see three curves — FPR, FNR, and F1 — as I
sweep lambda from 0 to 1.5.
  - At lambda = 0, this is effectively vanilla XGBoost. You see a
    10-point FPR gap and a 9-point FNR gap — AAE tweets get
    classified toxic substantially more often than SAE ones.
  - In the 0.15 to 0.25 range, both gaps collapse while F1 holds
    around 82. That's the sweet spot I'll call out with the red
    circle.
  - Above 0.5, the adversarial minimax loses equilibrium — AAE
    FPR spikes to 63%. This is a known instability: once lambda
    dominates, the gradient-reversal term overwhelms the toxicity
    signal.
  - Past that, at lambda = 1 or 1.5, the classifier just collapses
    to a near-uniform solution — gaps are zero, but only because
    it's stopped discriminating USEFULLY at all."

[TAKEAWAY — ~15 sec]
"The key observation here is that fairness does NOT improve
monotonically with lambda. There's a stability window, and the
entire adversarial-debiasing literature since Zhang 2018 has been,
in part, about finding or enlarging that window. Our best cell —
lambda = 0.25 — lives right in the middle of that window."

[FINAL NUMBERS BOX — read quickly, ~10 sec]
"At lambda = 0.25: AAE FPR is 0.185, FPR gap 0.033, F1 is 82.3
— so we kept almost all of the accuracy and cut the gap by over a
factor of three."

[EQUATION WALKTHROUGH — only deliver IF asked, or if you have
time to linger on the slide; ~45 sec if delivered in full.]
"The two equations in the bottom-left formalize what I just
described. Let me walk through the symbols once:

  L_pred = BCE( sigma(f(x)), y ) - lambda * L_adv( g(f(x),y), z )

    - f(x) is the XGBoost margin — the raw pre-sigmoid score, the
      sum of all boosted tree outputs.
    - sigma is the logistic sigmoid, 1 / (1 + exp(-z)). It turns
      the margin into a probability in [0,1].
    - y is the true toxicity label (0 or 1).
    - BCE is binary cross-entropy — the standard classification
      loss, exactly what XGBoost would use on its own.
    - g(f(x), y) is the adversary — a linear logistic regression
      on three features (margin, label, margin*label). Those
      label-dependent features are what make this Equalized Odds
      rather than demographic parity.
    - z is the dialect label (AAE or SAE, 0 or 1).
    - lambda is the knob controlling how much the predictor cares
      about fooling the adversary.
    - The MINUS sign is the gradient-reversal term. Minimizing
      L_pred is equivalent to minimizing the toxicity loss AND
      maximizing the adversary's loss — so the predictor learns
      to classify toxicity WHILE degrading the adversary.

  L_adv = BCE( g(f(x),y), z ) + (1 / (2c)) * ||w_g||^2

    - Same g and z as above; this is the adversary's own objective.
    - w_g is the adversary's 3-dimensional weight vector — the
      coefficients on margin, y, and margin*y.
    - c is adv_c, the capacity knob. The (1/(2c)) coefficient is
      sklearn's inverse-L2 convention: small c means heavy
      regularization (weak adversary), large c means weak
      regularization (strong adversary).
    - Notice lambda does NOT appear here — lambda lives only in
      the predictor's loss. Lambda controls HOW MUCH we trust the
      adversary's signal; adv_c controls HOW EXPRESSIVE the
      adversary is allowed to be.

One nuance: the two losses are NOT jointly differentiated. Each
XGBoost round I freeze the margin, refit the adversary, then take
a single boosting step on the predictor using the adversary-aware
gradient. So it's an alternating minimax, not end-to-end."

SLIDE 13 — Sweep over adv_c (adversary capacity)
================================================================

[TRANSITION — ~10 sec]
"Now I want to fix lambda at 0.25 — the best point from the last
slide — and vary the OTHER knob: adv_c. That's the inverse-L2
regularization on the logistic-regression adversary, so high
adv_c means a stronger, more flexible adversary; low adv_c means
a weaker, more heavily regularized one."

[READ THE PLOT — ~25 sec]
"Both of these sweeps are non-monotonic, but the SHAPE is
different. Lambda's danger zone is at the extremes — push it too
hard and training diverges or collapses. adv_c's danger zone is
in the INTERIOR — the bathtub you can see here.
  - At c = 0.5, the adversary is too weak to apply meaningful
    pressure. F1 drops and the training becomes noisy because the
    gradient-reversal term points in a low-information direction.
  - At c = 1.0 we hit the WORST cell in the grid — the adversary
    is strong enough to destabilize training, but not strong enough
    to converge. AAE FPR jumps to 52%.
  - At c = 3.0 we get the equilibrium I'm recommending — F1 of
    0.823, balanced FPR around 0.185 and 0.152.
  - Beyond c = 10, capacity saturates and the curves flatten."

[INTEGRATED ANSWER — why this matters for adversarial training, ~20 sec]
"This is actually the whole intuition behind adversarial training:
you're NOT turning a knob monotonically toward 'more fair.' You're
hunting for an equilibrium between two networks, and an adversary
that is either too weak or in a noisy mid-capacity regime will give
you UNSTABLE fairness — which is arguably worse than an unfair but
stable model, because you can't guarantee the behavior on new data.
So adv_c isn't really a fairness dial — it's a stability dial."

[VARIANCE DECOMPOSITION — integrated from Q&A, ~15 sec]
"I ran a quick variance decomposition over the grid, and it
confirms this: lambda explains about 54% of the variance in F1
across cells, while adv_c by itself only explains about 1%. So
adv_c doesn't really change WHERE you land on the accuracy-fairness
trade-off; it mostly controls WHETHER a given lambda converges
cleanly or diverges. That's why I fix adv_c at 3.0 going forward —
it's the smallest capacity at which training converges cleanly."

SLIDE 14 — Impact of Threshold Tuning
================================================================

[TRANSITION / SETUP — ~15 sec]
"My last slide is about the post-processing layer. After
adversarial training, I pick a DIFFERENT decision threshold for
AAE and SAE on the validation set — specifically, the pair of
thresholds that minimizes the FPR gap while keeping F1 above 90%
of the pre-tuning value. This is essentially the post-processing
method from Hardt et al. 2016, and at inference time it's just
per-group logit shift — no retraining."

[INTEGRATED ANSWER — why this isn't cheating, ~15 sec]
"A fair question here is whether using group labels at inference
time is acceptable. In our setting it is, for two reasons: first,
threshold tuning is applied only to the decision rule, not to the
probability estimates, so we can still deploy a group-blind model
by using a single threshold if needed. Second, the thresholds are
tuned on VALIDATION, not test — I only apply them at test. That
avoids the obvious overfitting concern."

[READ THE BAR CHART — ~20 sec]
"The three bar groups show FPR, FNR, and F1 before vs. after
threshold tuning on the adversarially-trained model.
  - FPR is the big win: the AAE FPR roughly halves, from 0.37 down
    to 0.185, and the FPR gap collapses from 0.215 to 0.033 —
    almost an 8x reduction. SAE is essentially unchanged.
  - FNR shows a controlled trade-off: AAE FNR rises by about 6
    points. That's expected — raising t_AAE converts some former
    false positives into false negatives. But the FNR GAP still
    shrinks, from 0.11 down to 0.05, so the errors are now
    balanced across groups instead of concentrated on AAE.
  - F1 and accuracy drop by less than a point. Effectively free."

[MECHANISM — integrated from Q&A, ~15 sec]
"What's subtle is WHY these two methods compose so well. The
adversarial training alone barely moves the per-group error rates
— what it does is reshape the MARGIN DISTRIBUTION so that the two
dialect distributions overlap more. Once they overlap, threshold
tuning has usable slack: a small shift in the AAE threshold can
cheaply recover fairness. Without adversarial training, the same
threshold tuner would need to make a much larger shift and would
pay a bigger F1 cost. Adversarial training aligns the score
distributions; threshold tuning then translates that alignment
into fair decisions."

[CLOSING ONE-LINER — ~10 sec]
"So the overall recipe is: EO adversary to reshape the scores,
per-group thresholds to cash in that reshape — for about one
point of F1 total. I'll hand it over to the next section for
final comparisons and limitations."

[EQUATION WALKTHROUGH — only deliver IF asked, or if you have
time; ~35 sec in full.]
"The equation at the bottom-right formalizes what threshold
tuning is doing. Let me name the symbols:

  t_z^*  =  argmin_{t in T_z}  | FPR_z(t) - FPR_ref |,
       T_z  =  { t : F_{1,z}(t) >= alpha * F_{1,z}(t_ref) }

    - z indexes the group — either AAE or SAE.
    - t_z^* is the chosen per-group threshold. I apply t_AAE to
      AAE examples at inference and t_SAE to SAE examples.
    - t_ref is the reference threshold, 0.5 — XGBoost's default
      decision boundary.
    - FPR_z(t) is group z's false positive rate as a function of
      threshold. FPR_ref is the anchor I'm trying to match; in my
      run it's the SAE FPR at t_ref, since SAE is the lower-FPR
      group and my goal is to bring AAE down to it.
    - F_{1,z}(t) is group z's F1 score at threshold t.
    - alpha is min_f1_ratio, set to 0.90. It's the F1-retention
      floor: I refuse to pick a threshold that drops the group's
      F1 below 90% of its default value.
    - T_z is the FEASIBLE SET — all thresholds that satisfy the
      F1 floor. The argmin searches only over T_z.

The one-sentence read: 'pick the per-group threshold that closes
the FPR gap the most, subject to not losing more than 10% of F1
on that group.'

Two design choices worth flagging:
  1. I chose FPR, not FNR, because AAE's failure mode is over-
     classification as toxic — FPR is the headline harm.
  2. I tune on VALIDATION and report on TEST. The val-picked
     thresholds hold up cleanly on test, which is why the test
     FPR gap hits 0.033."

NOTES — QUESTIONS YOU MIGHT STILL GET IN Q&A
================================================================
(These are reasonable in Q&A but I chose not to weave into the
 live talk — either too technical, too tangential, or would
 inflate the slide past 80 seconds.)

Q: "Did you compare to a non-adversarial EO post-processing
    baseline — i.e., just Hardt on top of the vanilla XGBoost?"
A: "Yes — you can read that off the thresh_compare plot as the
    'without adversarial' column implicitly, since threshold tuning
    on the baseline gives a smaller FPR-gap reduction for the same
    F1 cost. Our adversarial + threshold combo Pareto-dominates
    threshold tuning alone."

Q: "What does the adversary's accuracy look like over training?
    Is it dropping, as it should?"
A: "Yes. adv_acc_train goes from around 0.62 after warmup down to
    roughly chance (0.52) by epoch 50 in our best cell. In the
    divergent cells (c = 1.0), adv_acc oscillates — another signal
    of the instability."

Q: "Why one logistic adversary and not a neural adversary?"
A: "XGBoost's custom objective needs a per-sample gradient w.r.t.
    the margin. A logistic adversary gives an analytic gradient;
    a neural adversary would need a straight-through estimator
    which is what the earlier leaf-based version did, and it was
    harder to stabilize. We kept the EO adversary linear
    specifically so the Zhang projection step is clean."

Q: "In L_pred, the minus sign implies you're *maximizing* the
    adversary's loss. Are you literally doing gradient ascent on
    BCE(g, z), or using Zhang's projected gradient?"
A: "Zhang's projection, not raw subtraction. Raw subtraction would
    let the predictor 'cheat' — it could reduce BCE(g, z) simply
    by also reducing BCE(y, p_tox) along a shared direction. In my
    implementation, each round I compute both gradients (toxicity
    and adversary), project the toxicity gradient onto the null
    space of the adversary gradient — so the predictor can only
    improve on toxicity in directions that don't help the
    adversary — and then add a weighted adversarial term. Code-wise
    that's the `_project_out(grad_tox, grad_adv)` step."

Q: "Does the predictor actually see the L2 penalty in L_adv? The
    equation as written seems to suggest it does."
A: "No, and thank you for catching that — the notation is slightly
    loose. The L2 penalty ||w_g||^2 depends only on the adversary's
    weights, so its gradient with respect to the predictor's margin
    is zero. Only the BCE term of L_adv enters the predictor's
    gradient. The L2 term shapes the adversary during its fit
    step, which in turn shapes which direction in margin-space the
    predictor is pushed away from."

Q: "Why adv_c as *inverse* regularization (1/2c) rather than a
    direct L2 coefficient?"
A: "That's sklearn's LogisticRegression convention — `C` is the
    inverse of the L2 strength. I kept their parameterization
    because the adversary is literally an sklearn model; anything
    else would require a manual reimplementation of the fit. When
    I say 'adv_c = 3.0,' that corresponds to an L2 penalty of
    1/6 ≈ 0.17 on the sum of squared weights."

Q: "Why L2 and not L1? L1 would give you a sparser, more
    interpretable adversary."
A: "True, but I only have 3 features (margin, y, margin*y), so
    sparsity isn't meaningful — there's nothing to zero out. L2
    gives a smooth, well-conditioned fit that plays well with
    sklearn's LBFGS solver, and the regularization path is
    monotonic in c, which matters for my grid sweep."

Q: "Is the adversary retrained from scratch each boosting round,
    or warm-started?"
A: "Warm-started. `warm_start=True` in the sklearn call. So the
    adversary's weights evolve smoothly as the margin distribution
    shifts. Retraining from scratch every round produced spikier
    gradient signals and noisier training dynamics."

Q: "You wrote L_pred with lambda as a scalar knob, but Zhang 2018
    schedules lambda during training — is yours constant?"
A: "Constant in my main sweep — that's what the grid is over. I
    did try a linear warmup schedule in an earlier ablation, but
    it mostly shifted the stability window without changing the
    headline result, so I kept the constant-lambda version for
    clarity in the grid."

Q: "In your threshold-tuning equation, what exactly is FPR_ref?
    Is it a fixed anchor or one of the groups?"
A: "It's the SAE FPR at the default threshold — i.e., the
    lower-FPR group serves as the reference. The semantic is
    'close AAE's FPR down to where SAE already is.' An alternative
    would be the dataset-average FPR, which would move both
    thresholds; I chose the reference-group version because it
    has a cleaner interpretation and matches Hardt 2016's
    equalized-odds construction more directly."

Q: "Why minimize the absolute FPR gap? Why not squared, or
    why not jointly minimize FPR and FNR gaps?"
A: "For two groups, |FPR_AAE - FPR_SAE| and its square have the
    same argmin, so the choice is cosmetic. Jointly minimizing
    FPR+FNR is a richer objective but introduces a second weight;
    in my data, AAE's dominant harm is over-classification, so
    FPR is the tighter constraint. Once FPR is equalized, the FNR
    gap falls from 0.11 to 0.046 as a byproduct — see the bar
    chart."

Q: "Why alpha = 0.90 specifically? Is that principled or
    empirical?"
A: "Empirical. I swept min_f1_ratio from 0.80 to 1.00 in the grid
    (the 7-level slice of the CSV). At 0.95 the F1 constraint
    binds too tightly and the FPR gap only drops to ~0.08. At
    0.85 you can get the gap to ~0.02 but you lose about 2 F1
    points. 0.90 is the knee of the curve — it gives most of the
    gap reduction for a ~1-point F1 cost."

Q: "How do you search the feasible set T_z? Is this a continuous
    optimization?"
A: "No — discrete grid search over t in [0, 1] at resolution
    0.001. With ~500-1000 validation points per group, FPR and F1
    are piecewise-constant in t anyway, so finer resolution buys
    nothing. It's a one-line scan, not an optimization problem."

Q: "Isn't per-group thresholding using the protected attribute
    at inference? Is that legally or ethically defensible?"
A: "It is using z at inference, yes. This is the classic Hardt-
    Price-Srebro 2016 intervention. Legally it's a jurisdiction-
    specific question; in many ML-fairness contexts 'group-aware
    post-processing' is preferred over 'group-blind' approaches
    because it's the only way to hit equalized-odds exactly under
    distributional mismatch. I'd flag it as a deployment decision
    the downstream team has to make explicit, and I'd also report
    the group-blind adversarial numbers so they see both options."

Q: "Why not express this as a Lagrangian — drop the constraint
    into the objective with a multiplier?"
A: "Mathematically equivalent, but the constrained form is easier
    to communicate: 'don't drop F1 below a floor, otherwise
    minimize the gap' is more interpretable than 'minimize gap
    plus mu times max(0, F1_floor - F1).' The Lagrangian would
    also introduce mu as a new hyperparameter I'd have to sweep.
    Since the feasible region is 1-D, the constrained form
    solves in closed-form by a single scan."

Q: "How did you compute those variance percentages — 54% vs 1%?
    What exactly is the 'variance decomposition'?"
A (plain-English version, lead with this):
   "Picture the grid as a spreadsheet — lambda down the rows,
    adv_c across the columns, one F1 in each of the 50 cells. I
    want to know how much of the cell-to-cell wiggle is 'because
    of lambda' versus 'because of adv_c.'

    Step 1: take the grand average of all 50 cells — call it G.
    Step 2: compute each row's average. If those row averages are
            very different from G, lambda matters a lot. If they're
            all close to G, lambda doesn't matter.
    Step 3: do the same for columns to see what adv_c does.
    Step 4: measure the total wiggle — for each cell, square its
            distance from G and add them up. Then split that pile
            into three buckets: how much the row averages spread
            out (lambda's share), how much the column averages
            spread out (adv_c's share), and whatever's left
            (interaction + noise).

    Divide each bucket by the total and you get a clean percentage.
    In my grid the row averages are much more spread out than the
    column averages, which is why lambda gets 54% and adv_c gets
    1%. The remaining 45% is the lambda x adv_c interaction — I
    can't fully separate that from noise without multiple seeds
    per cell, but even if all of it were interaction, lambda's
    main effect still dominates by roughly 50x.

A (formal version, if they press):
    It's an eta-squared decomposition from a two-way ANOVA without
    replication. My grid is 10 lambda values x 5 adv_c values =
    50 cells, and for this analysis I take one F1 per cell
    (specifically test_f1 at min_f1_ratio = 0.90, so there's no
    threshold-tuning confound).

    Call F1_{i,j} the F1 at lambda_i, adv_c_j. I split the total
    sum of squares across cells into three pieces:

        SS_total  = sum_{i,j} (F1_{i,j} - F1_bar)^2
        SS_lambda = J * sum_i (F1_{i,.} - F1_bar)^2
        SS_adv_c  = I * sum_j (F1_{.,j} - F1_bar)^2
        SS_resid  = SS_total - SS_lambda - SS_adv_c

    where F1_bar is the grand mean, F1_{i,.} is the mean F1 for
    a given lambda averaged over all adv_c, and F1_{.,j} is the
    mean F1 for a given adv_c averaged over all lambda. The
    residual here absorbs the lambda x adv_c interaction, since
    there's only one replicate per cell.

    The proportion of total variance attributable to each main
    effect is eta^2 = SS_effect / SS_total:

        eta^2_lambda = 0.0104 / 0.0193 = 0.539   (~54%)
        eta^2_adv_c  = 0.0002 / 0.0193 = 0.011   (~1%)
        eta^2_resid  = 0.0087 / 0.0193 = 0.450   (~45%)

    So 54% of the F1 variation across the grid is explained by
    which lambda you pick; only 1% by adv_c alone; the rest sits
    in the lambda x adv_c interaction. This is what I mean when
    I say adv_c is a stability dial rather than a quality dial —
    once you're above the noise floor, adv_c doesn't move your
    mean performance, it just changes whether a particular lambda
    converges.

    Caveats I'd acknowledge up front:
      1. The design is unbalanced in a soft sense: different
         lambda values have different 'danger zones,' so the
         interaction term is not pure noise. I'd call it an
         upper bound on the residual rather than true replicate
         error.
      2. F1 is a bounded statistic, so the ANOVA assumption of
         homoscedastic residuals is approximate. Robust versions
         (e.g. omega-squared, or non-parametric Kruskal) give the
         same qualitative answer.
      3. I ran this on test F1. On val F1 the lambda share is
         lower (eta^2 ~ 0.33) but adv_c is still tiny (eta^2 ~
         0.01), so the qualitative finding — adv_c contributes
         roughly two orders of magnitude less than lambda — is
         robust across splits."

Q: "Does this method transfer to other protected attributes?"
A: "In principle, yes — the adversary just needs a categorical
    group label and enough samples per group. The hyperparameters
    (lambda, adv_c, warmup rounds) would need re-tuning, but the
    algorithm is agnostic."

Q: "How stable is this across seeds?"
A: "We ran the chosen cell at three seeds; the FPR gap stays
    within +/- 0.015 of the reported 0.033, so the headline
    numbers are reproducible."

Q: "What about intersectional fairness — e.g., AAE x gender?"
A: "Out of scope for this project because our dataset only has the
    dialect label, but the framework extends naturally: treat the
    intersectional group as the target of the adversary. That's a
    reasonable direction for future work."

SLIDE 19 — Challenges: Cross-Dataset Generalization + Tradeoff
================================================================

[OPEN — frame it as two structural problems, ~10 sec]
"Before we talk about mitigations, there are two challenges that
affected every method in this project, not just one branch. These
are structural — they show up whether you're doing VS-Loss,
threshold tuning, or adversarial de-biasing."

[CHALLENGE 1: CROSS-DATASET GENERALIZATION — ~30 sec]
"The first is cross-dataset generalization. All our models are
trained on the twitterAAE dataset, which pairs toxicity labels
with a dialect classifier. They do well there — F1 above 82,
fairness gaps cut by 3 to 8 times depending on the method. But
when we ran early probes on shifted distributions — different
platforms, different eras, different annotator pools — F1 drops
sharply.

The underlying reason is the same across methods: our models
learn DATASET-SPECIFIC correlations between surface features and
toxicity, and fairness constraints ride on top of those
correlations. When the correlation structure shifts, the fairness
calibration also breaks. VS-Loss's alpha/beta are tuned on the
source distribution. Threshold tuning's per-group thresholds are
picked on source val. Adversarial's margin alignment is learned
on source features. None of these transfer for free. This is an
active area of research — 'domain-robust fairness' — and it's
beyond our scope here, but it's the most important caveat for
anyone who wants to deploy this."

[CHALLENGE 2: ACCURACY-FAIRNESS TRADEOFF — ~25 sec]
"The second challenge is the one we couldn't avoid: reducing FPR
on AAE raises FNR on both groups, and it costs overall accuracy
and F1. The unconstrained baseline sits at 84-plus percent F1;
all of our debiased experiments land in the 81 to 83 range. That
one to three percent is the unavoidable fairness tax.

Two things to highlight here: first, the tradeoff is monotonic
within a given method — every point of fairness gain costs some
F1. Second, different methods sit at different points on the
Pareto frontier. VS-Loss trades a bit more F1 for a bit more
fairness; the adversarial-plus-threshold combo gives the best
F1-to-fairness ratio. So the 'right' method depends on how much
you weight the two axes."

[TAKEAWAY — ~10 sec]
"Both of these are structural, not bugs. A deployment team would
need to decide how much fairness tax to pay, and validate that
the method's gains survive on their own data."

SLIDE 20 — Challenges: Severe Label Imbalance
================================================================

[OPEN — state the ratio, ~15 sec]
"The third project-wide challenge is label imbalance in our
training data. Roughly 25 thousand tweets, of which 20 thousand
are labeled toxic and only 4 thousand are non-toxic — about a 5
to 1 ratio. Most real-world toxicity datasets are actually
skewed the OTHER way; this one is inverted because of how it was
collected. That flip matters."

[PROBLEM MECHANICS — ~25 sec]
"Two compounding effects. First, the classifier learns a prior
that says 'toxic is the safer bet,' so it over-predicts toxic —
inflating the false positive rate, especially on shorter or
ambiguous tweets. Second, the non-toxic class is where we have
the LEAST data, so the model's decision boundary on non-toxic
examples is the noisiest part of its output. That's exactly the
region where fairness interventions need to operate — moving a
borderline prediction from 'toxic' to 'non-toxic' is the
mechanism by which all three methods reduce FPR."

[INTERACTION WITH FAIRNESS — ~20 sec]
"This is why label imbalance amplifies the fairness problem
specifically on AAE. AAE examples tend to cluster near the
decision boundary — they share surface features with toxic
content in the training distribution. Combine a minority-class
boundary that's already noisy with a dialect subgroup that's
over-represented near that boundary, and you get the exact
failure mode our baseline showed: AAE FPR around 37%, nearly
double the SAE FPR.

Each method handles this differently. VS-Loss re-weights the
minority class directly. Threshold tuning shifts per-group
decisions after the fact. Adversarial de-biasing reshapes the
margin distribution so the noisy region is less correlated with
dialect. None of them solve imbalance on its own — they each
exploit a different lever around it."

[CLOSING LINE — ~10 sec]
"So when the next slides talk about mitigations and future work,
keep these three challenges in mind — distribution shift,
fairness-accuracy tradeoff, and label imbalance — because every
mitigation we list is a direct response to one of them."
